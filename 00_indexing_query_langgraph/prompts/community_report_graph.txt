# Report Structure
The report should include the following sections:
- TITLE: community's name that represents its key entities - title should be short but specific. When possible, include representative named entities in the title.
- SUMMARY: An executive summary of the community's overall structure, how its entities are related to each other, and significant information associated with its entities.
- REPORT RATING: # Importance Criteria

A float score between 0–10 that represents how relevant and significant the provided text is to Enterprise IT infrastructure & support (IT operations, networking, identity/authentication, and Microsoft services) from the perspective of an enterprise infrastructure architect and community analyst. The score reflects the document's operational impact, technical actionability, security and compliance implications, value to architecture/runbooks/blueprints, and relevance for stakeholder coordination and escalation.

Considerations used to evaluate and weight the score:
- Operational impact: scope of affected users/systems (single user vs. entire tenant/multiple sites), production vs. test/UAT, service availability and business continuity implications.
- Urgency / severity: whether the issue requires immediate mitigation (outage, data loss, security breach) or is informational/low-priority.
- Root-cause clarity and reproducibility: presence of clear diagnostics, logs, reproducible steps, and confirmed root cause versus vague symptoms.
- Technical domain relevance: direct relevance to networking, identity/authentication (AD/Azure AD, ADFS, MFA, tokens), Microsoft 365/Azure services, Direct Routing/Teams media flow, certificate services/NDES, reverse proxy/WAF, and other core infrastructure components.
- Security and compliance exposure: privileged account misuse, misconfigurations causing authentication failures, TLS/certificate problems, firewall/WAF rules that could expose or block traffic, or deviations from security blueprints.
- Remediation detail and outcomes: existence of documented fixes, configuration changes, workarounds, or blueprint updates; whether change is temporary or permanent.
- Recurrence and trend value: indication of recurring faults or systemic issues that warrant architecture or process change (versus one-off incidents).
- Knowledge and governance value: usefulness for runbooks, playbooks, architectural blueprints, monitoring/alert tuning, and cross-team procedures; mentions of decisions, templates, or standards.
- Collaboration and escalation needs: clear stakeholders, handoffs, ownership changes, and cross-team coordination requirements.
- Confidentiality/security sensitivity: whether technical details are safe to document openly or restricted for security reasons (affects usability).

Interpretation guide (examples of score bands):
- 0.0–1.9: Irrelevant, spam, or unrelated to enterprise IT operations and architectures.
- 2.0–4.9: Low relevance—simple, local user issues with trivial impact or insufficient technical detail.
- 5.0–6.9: Moderate relevance—useful operational incidents with clear fix but limited scope or without broader architectural implications.
- 7.0–8.9: High relevance—production-impacting incidents, security-sensitive misconfigurations, cross-team fixes, or material deviations from blueprints that require coordinated remediation and follow-up.
- 9.0–10.0: Critical—widespread outages, security breaches, systemic architectural failures, or policy/blueprint violations that demand immediate escalation, cross-functional incident response, and lasting architecture or governance changes.
